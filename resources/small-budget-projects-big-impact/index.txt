		<p>What can you do for $500 or less?</p>
<ul>
<li>Make a few groovy <a href="http://time.com/money/3742862/best-affordable-home-improvements-upgrades/">DIY home improvements</a></li>
<li>Dine on some (really) <a href="https://www.buzzfeed.com/stevenlim/we-had-the-fried-chicken-of-your-dreams-ranging-fr?utm_term=.su8X5jWPD2#.dmA8OvRg9W">fancy fried chicken</a></li>
<li><a href="https://www.ioby.org/blog/small-is-the-new-big-10-projects-you-can-do-for-around-500">Start a game-changing project in your neighborhood</a></li>
</ul>
<p>$500 may not seem like a huge amount of money, but in the <a href="https://www.ioby.org/10YAstories">10 years we’ve been helping local leaders fundraise for community projects</a> we’ve seen just how much of an impact even small budget projects can make. There’s a reason the phrase “throwing money at the problem” has developed a negative connotation, after all. At the end of the day, it’s simply a matter of how your project gets done. Here are a few of the top low-budget best practices we’ve learned from our local leaders in the past decade.</p>
<p><span></span></p>
<h2>How to Get Big Results from a Small-Budget Project<br>
<a href="https://ioby-cms-assets.nyc3.cdn.digitaloceanspaces.com/fa0511ec-841e-4420-a8f5-25d89bfc3726.png"><img height="762" width="1024" alt="Small budgets, big impact" src="https://ioby-cms-assets.nyc3.cdn.digitaloceanspaces.com/fa0511ec-841e-4420-a8f5-25d89bfc3726.png"></a></h2>
<p><em>Crosswalk flags, like these placed by ioby leader Sarah Newstok in Memphis, don’t cost much but have a big impact on safety.</em></p>
<h3>1) Make a visible change that shows you care—and that others should, too</h3>
<p>Example: <a href="https://www.ioby.org/blog/learn-from-a-leader-help-peds-walk-safely-with-crosswalk-flags">Crosswalk Flags</a></p>
<p>Memphian Sarah Newstok <a href="https://www.ioby.org/project/crosswalk-flags">raised $543 on ioby</a> to install crosswalk flags at a busy intersection in her neighborhood that drivers routinely sped through, scaring and endangering pedestrians. Now, before peds cross the street, they can pick up a brightly-colored, reusable flag from a bucket zip-tied to a nearby pole. They wave the flag while crossing for increased visibility, then deposit it on the other side for the next person.</p>
<p>After watching the flags in action for a while, Sarah reported that the city took notice: “The flags are not a perfect solution, but I do think they help. We did get a crosswalk and a crosswalk sign at our intersection after installing the flags, so that’s progress!”</p>
<h3>2) Build in a “pay it forward” aspect that amplifies good work</h3>
<p>Example: <a href="https://www.ioby.org/project/banish-bead">Banish the Bead!</a></p>
<p>A group of students at <a href="http://www.whitehallschools.net/ourschools/middleschool/">Whitehall Middle School</a> in Whitehall, Michigan wrote and self-published a children’s book about the negative impacts of microbead pollution in the Great Lakes. The young scholars titled the book Billy the Bluegill and the Microbead Mishap and distributed it for free to all 81 third grade classrooms in their county. The students said they believed that if they could educate younger kids about how their consumer choices make a difference to the environment, their parents would also get the message.</p>
<p>For the low price tag of $572—and some help from campaigns on <a href="https://www.facebook.com/Billythebluegill/">Facebook</a> and <a href="https://twitter.com/beadbanishers">Twitter</a>—the <a href="http://www.blurb.com/b/6016176-billy-the-bluegill-and-the-microbead-mishap">book became available nationwide</a>. In the same year, two Michigan senators <a href="https://www.freep.com/story/news/local/michigan/2015/05/26/senate-microbead-bill/27970933/">proposed legislation which would phase out the use of microbeads</a>. Coincidence?</p>
<p><img srcset="https://blog.ioby.org/wp-content/uploads/2018/08/10818258_1559379324312837_405932643645684222_o.jpg 2048w, https://blog.ioby.org/wp-content/uploads/2018/08/10818258_1559379324312837_405932643645684222_o-300x225.jpg 300w, https://blog.ioby.org/wp-content/uploads/2018/08/10818258_1559379324312837_405932643645684222_o-768x576.jpg 768w, https://blog.ioby.org/wp-content/uploads/2018/08/10818258_1559379324312837_405932643645684222_o-1024x768.jpg 1024w" height="1536" width="2048" alt="Ban Microbeads" src="https://ioby-cms-assets.nyc3.cdn.digitaloceanspaces.com/1ace377d-e398-406c-b3e6-21a38e21433b.jpg" loading="lazy"><em>Students at Ealy Elementary School reading a book written by their peers at Whitehall Middle School about the negative impacts of microbeads.</em></p>
<h3>3) Try a project that can be renewed or repeated for ongoing gains</h3>
<p>Example: <a href="https://www.ioby.org/project/pumpkin-carving-families-cleveland">Pumpkin Carving with Families in Cleveland</a></p>
<p>In the words of project leader Austin B, “One of the things that can make a neighborhood safer is when neighbors know one another and can look out for each other.” Austin’s project set out to connect and strengthen relationships between as many neighbors as possible by giving people from all backgrounds a fun reason to come together: carving pumpkins to decorate their street.</p>
<p>By pooling $328, Austin’s neighbors got a plethora of pumpkins, all the necessary carving supplies, and tasty refreshments. We’d bet they had some fun and made some new friends, too. And at such a price, they could hold a carving day every fall.</p>
<h3>4) Go for ideas that offer multiple bangs for their buck</h3>
<p>Example: <a href="https://www.ioby.org/project/solar-lights-mssa">Solar Lights for MSSA</a></p>
<p>When <a href="https://www.memphissteamacademy.org/">Maxine Smith STEAM Academy</a>, a Memphis middle school, was in its first year of operation, a group of parents, students, and staff rallied to brighten up the front of their building. They decided to do so using solar-powered flood lights—an inexpensive and green option—and to install them on an already-scheduled school clean-up day.</p>
<p>The result? Increased safety and nicer aesthetics—with a side of team building—all for $515.</p>

<h3>5) Make something with your neighbors that can serve the whole community</h3>
<p>Example: <a href="https://www.ioby.org/project/yard-yard-birds">A yard for the yard birds</a></p>
<p>Brooklyn’s <a href="http://www.greeneacresgarden.com/">Green Acres Community Garden</a> has a large membership—and even more admirers. Everyone who passes by can enjoy the pleasing sights of the fruits, vegetables, flowers, herbs, goldfish, and chickens they tend. The garden raised $273 on ioby to buy the necessary materials (chicken wire, paint, screws, etc) to expand the hens’ small yard, giving them—and their fans—a larger, more comfortable, and cleaner space to eat, play, and socialize in.</p>
<p>According to project leader Elizabeth L, “Greene Acres is a beautiful oasis where neighbors commune with nature and practice organic gardening. Our chickens are beloved by members and visitors who hold them, feed them greens, and learn about caring for animals and growing food ethically and sustainably. Our girls attract many visitors of all ages and backgrounds to the garden; [now they have] a haven of their own.”</p>
