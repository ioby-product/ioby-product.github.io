17:T86e,<p><em><span>What happens to all of the </span><a href="http://blogs.smithsonianmag.com/science/2014/01/what-happens-to-all-the-salt-we-dump-on-the-roads/">salt placed on the roads in winter</a> -- does it pollute our drinking water, or harm local wildlife? How do we know if there are <a href="http://mysticriver.org/cyano/">unsafe levels of waterborne bacteria</a> in the river?</em></p>
<p><span>The </span><a href="http://mysticriver.org/">Mystic River</a> faces serious water quality challenges, including leaky sewer pipes, waste disposal sites, fuel hydrocarbons, and road salt; it received a ‘D’ on its <a href="http://www.epa.gov/mysticriver/reportcards.html">2012 US EPA water quality report card</a>. The Alewife Brook subwatershed is one of the most contaminated water bodies in Boston.</p>
<p>Current water quality sensors cost hundreds (often thousands) of dollars, and use proprietary data formats -- making widespread monitoring expensive and difficult. It’s hard for local residents to gather data on their own.</p>
<p><span>With your help, we’re going to develop</span> an ‘open source’ water quality monitoring platform that will cost far less, and will be open for communities everywhere to build and use.</p>
<p><span>The </span><a href="http://publiclab.org">Public Lab community</a> is partnering with the Mystic River Watershed Association, the Massachusetts Water Resources Research Center, Plymouth State, and UNH to develop:</p>
<ul>
	<li>
		<span>a low-cost, DIY, </span><a href="http://publiclab.org/wiki/mystic-river">open source water quality monitoring sensor</a></li>
	<li>
		<span>an </span>online ‘open data’ water quality repository</li>
	<li>
		<span>two </span>grassroots water quality workshops</li>
	<li>
		<span>a </span>summer program for local students</li>
</ul>
<p><span>To be successful, we’ll need your support. Donations go to Public Lab, a 501(c)(3) non-profit.</span></p>
<p><span>We’ll use any funds we receive beyond our goal to deploy additional sensors in the Mystic River watershed and at other sites in Massachusetts, New Hampshire, and New Orleans.</span></p>
18:T4eb,<p><span>The Mystic River in Massachusetts flows from the Mystic Lakes in Winchester and Arlington, through Medford, Somerville, Everett, Charlestown and Chelsea, and into Boston Harbor, and has supported a long history of economic progress in one of the most densely populated urban areas of New England. Today, portions of the watershed often fail to meet state bacteria standards for swimming and boating. Several organizations are engaged in water monitoring projects for the Mystic, but the high cost and ‘closed data’ nature of current technology severely limits the scope of current efforts, and makes data sharing difficult. Public Lab has extensive experience developing open platforms for </span><a href="http://publiclab.org/wiki/balloon-mapping">low-cost aerial imagery</a>, <a href="http://publiclab.org/wiki/spectrometer">DIY spectroscopy</a>, and <a href="http://publiclab.org/wiki/near-infrared-camera">infrared plant health analysis</a>; by designing a low-cost, ‘open source’ water quality monitor that is easy to build and maintain, we’ll greatly expand the scope of current monitoring efforts, and enable communities to develop their own grassroots monitoring networks.</p>

We'll also be relying on collaborators at UNH, Plymouth State, UMass Amherst and the Mystic River Watershed Association to deploy our open hardware monitors alongside their own state-of-the-art, commercial sensors, as a way of calibrating and comparing the accuracy and reliability of our prototypes. </span></span></p>

<p><span>- a summer water quality educational workshop for around ten students;</span></p>
